Texas A&M University

Associate Research Scientist (Post-Doc)

2014-2016

For many centuries, biologists have been attempting to understand and model cellular interactions in the human body that is the key to diagnostics and therapeutics. Despite the many successful discoveries in the past, there still exist challenging open problems in the field of biology. Due to the ample complexity and uncertainty in these problems, promising solutions can not be found unless powerful computational tools are employed. Thus, a critical goal of computational biology is to model the dynamics of biological networks describing how genes, proteins, chemicals and so on evolve over time. In this scholarly work, I proposed a new hybrid technique for dynamical modeling of genomic networks. This method deals with each interaction based on its biological nature; hence, the generated model effectively captures the system dynamical behavior. I also introduced the concept of "dynamics uncertainty" that is due to our limited biological knowledge. In general, model uncertainty is not desirable as it can substantially decrease the design performance. To address this, we developed an optimal objective-based Bayesian experimental design method to quantify and reduce uncertainty in dynamical models. For practical implementations, I successfully applied these new methods to the database of human pathways and generated reliable dynamical models.

Programming: Python, XML, MATLAB
