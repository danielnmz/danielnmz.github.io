Texas A&M University

Graduate Research Assistant (PhD)

2010-2014

My PhD research developed new design methods in control systems. In my first work, I studied algebraic geometry and applied elimination theory and Groebner basis to compute stabilizing controllers of fixed structure. In another research, I used convex optimization techniques to solve the problem of designing controllers guaranteeing desired system transient response. I proved that in the case of PID controllers such set of desirable controllers can be obtained by solving a set of Semi-Definite Programs (SDPs). In linear systems theory, we took a different path and introduced a new design philosophy. In contrast to traditional design methods which require a mathematical model of the system in the form of algebraic or differential equations, our proposed methodology assumes no mathematical models are available, nor are they required, and determines the design parameters directly from a small set of measurements. The interdisciplinary nature and wide range of applications that this approach embraces promises the beginnings of a new era in engineering design. These results are published in our book "Linear Systems: A Measurement Based Approach" by Springer. Besides my theoretical research activities, I established the Control Engineering and Design laboratory course for the first time in the Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering at Texas A&M University. The experiments accommodate classical and modern control design techniques on systems such as servomotors, flexible link structures, and inverted pendulums.
